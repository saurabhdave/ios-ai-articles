Opening

Swift macros have arrived in earnest and teams are quietly deciding whether to treat them like a productivity tool or a new piece of build infrastructure. They promise to eliminate repetitive plumbing, but they also change build behavior, diagnostics, and rollout dynamics in ways your QA cycle and CI need to understand. If you care about predictable releases, you need a plan for introducing macros that isn’t just “add and hope.”

This Week's Big Story

Swift 6.3 Macros for iOS Codebases

Macros can generate large swaths of deterministic boilerplate at compile time, but that very determinism makes them a risky dependency: a small macro change can ripple through every client and alter builds. The question isn’t whether to use macros — it’s where to confine them so they buy you maintenance savings without turning into invisible, version-sensitive compiler services. Read on for practical rollout, testing, and operational rules that keep macros useful and predictable.
